01 / The Study

Framework

A case study in North Rhine-Westphalia examining how forest disturbances drive above-ground biomass loss across space and time.

01
🌲📉
The Problem

Between 2018 and 2022, over 490 km² of German forests were damaged by drought, bark beetle, and logging cascades. Yet disturbance impacts and biomass dynamics are often analysed separately, limiting understanding of how different disturbance types drive carbon loss over time and space.

02
🛰️
The Approach

Fuse GEDI LiDAR + Sentinel-1 SAR + Sentinel-2 optical + terrain + climate data into a 100 m annual grid. Model above-ground biomass with RF, kNN, and CNN. Map four disturbance types. Then run a Linear Mixed Effects Model to attribute which disturbance is actually driving biomass loss.

03
💡
The Core Finding

ΔAGB is a lagging indicator of cumulative stress, not an immediate disturbance response. Biomass loss doesn't peak in the year the disturbance occurs. It accumulates over subsequent years. Same-year accounting systematically underestimates what forests are actually losing.

A Multi-Sensor ETL & Feature Engineering pipeline

Reproducible workflow integrating data fusion, machine learning, and statistical attribution to derive disturbance-driven AGB dynamics.

🛰️
Multi-Sensor Data Fusion

GEDI LiDAR footprints fused with annual Sentinel-1/2 composites, GLCM texture features, canopy height model, SRTM terrain, and ERA5 climate variables, harmonised on a 100 m grid.

Core Finding · LMEM Attribution

ΔAGB Is a Lagging Indicator: Drought Starts the Cascade

The LMEM confirmed drought as the dominant negative driver of biomass change (β = −5.875). AGB loss doesn't spike in the drought year, it accumulates in subsequent years as bark beetle mortality progresses and salvage logging follows. Same-year accounting underestimates AGB loss. This has direct implications for LULUCF carbon reporting under EU climate policy.

Policy Implication

Annual same-year LULUCF carbon accounting under EU climate reporting frameworks systematically underestimates forest carbon loss. ΔAGB should be tracked as a multi-year lagged response to disturbance events, not as a co-temporal indicator.

Scientific Contributions

What this work advances

First integrated AGB–disturbance framework for NRW at 100 m annual scale

Combines GEDI-derived biomass prediction with four concurrent disturbance proxies in a unified visualization framework. Enables attribution of which disturbance type drives local carbon loss each year.

SAR-optical fusion stabilises prediction across cloud-affected years

Sentinel-1 VV/VH reduces canopy saturation effects and cloud-gap errors that compromise optical-only models in Germany's frequently overcast temperate climate.

Evidence of drought → pest → logging disturbance cascades

Spatial co-occurrence analysis reveals that NRW's biomass-loss hotspots concentrate along drought-preconditioned spruce corridors where beetle outbreaks then triggered salvage operations.

Interactive GEE dashboard for evidence-based forest management

Publicly accessible tool allowing foresters and policymakers to query disturbance footprints, visualise biomass trajectories, and identify hotspot zones, no GIS software required.
