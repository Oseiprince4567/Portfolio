The Problem

Between 2018 and 2022, over 490 km² of German forests were damaged by drought, bark beetle, and logging cascades. Yet, disturbance impacts and biomass dynamics are often analysed separately, which limits understanding of how disturbance types, severities, and frequencies drive biomass loss over time and space. Using North Rhine-Westphalia (NRW) as case study, this thesis develops a spatio-temporal remote-sensing framework to (i) predict annual above-ground biomass (AGB) for 2019-2022, (ii) map and characterise drought, fire, logging and pest disturbances, and (iii) quantify disturbance-driven above-ground biomass change.

02
🛰️
The Approach

Fuse GEDI LiDAR + Sentinel-1 SAR + Sentinel-2 optical + terrain + climate data into a 100 m annual grid. Model and predict Above-ground biomass with RF, kNN and CNN algorithms. Map four disturbance types. Then run a Linear Mixed Effects Model to attribute which disturbance type is actually driving biomass loss.

03
💡
The Insight

ΔAGB is a lagging indicator of cumulative stress rather than an immediate response to a disturbance type. Biomass loss doesn't peak in the year of disturbance occurence. It accumulates over subsequent years. Same-year disturbance correlations systematically underestimate what forests are actually losing.

Methodology

A six-step pipeline

From satellite imagery and remote sensing data to a live interactive dashboard. Every step reproducible in Google Earth Engine.

STEP 01

🛰️ Multi-Sensor Fusion

GEDI footprints fused with annual Sentinel-1/2 composites, GLCM textures, canopy height, SRTM terrain, and ERA5 climate, harmonised on a 100m grid.

STEP 02

🤖 ML AGB Modelling

RF, kNN, CNN trained under GroupKfold cross-validation. RF selected with R²=0.959. SHAP used to rank which satellite bands actually explain biomass variance.

STEP 03

🗺️ Annual AGB Maps

RF generated wall-to-wall above-ground biomass maps for 2019, 2020, 2021, 2022. Providing four consecutive annual biomass rasters validated against ESA-CCI and DLR products.

STEP 04

🔥 Disturbance Δ-Layers

Annual change layers for drought, fire, pest, and logging computed from spectral indices. Disturbance Type-specific quantile thresholds suppress false positives while preserving true signals.

📐 LMEM Attribution

Pixel-wise correlation of ΔAGB and disturbance layers with a Linear Mixed Effects Model. Fixed effects = disturbance type severities. Random effect = Biomass baseline year. Drought β=−5.875.

Core Finding · LMEM Attribution

ΔAGB Is a Lagging Indicator. Drought Starts the Cascade

The LMEM confirmed drought as the dominant negative driver of biomass change (β=−5.875). AGB loss doesn't spike in the drought year, it accumulates in subsequent years as bark beetle mortality progresses and salvage logging follows. Same-year accounting undestimates AGB loss. This has direct implications for LULUCF carbon reporting under EU climate policy.

Scientific Contributions

What this work advances

First integrated AGB–disturbance framework for NRW at 100 m annual scale

Combines GEDI-derived biomass prediction with four concurrent disturbance proxies in a visualization framework. This enables attribution of which disturbance type drives local carbon loss each year.

SAR-optical fusion stabilises prediction across cloud-affected years

Sentinel-1 VV/VH reduces canopy saturation effects and cloud-gap errors that compromise optical-only models in Germany's frequently overcast temperate climate.

Evidence of drought → pest → logging disturbance cascades

Spatial co-occurrence analysis reveals that NRW's biomass-loss hotspots concentrate along drought-preconditioned spruce corridors where beetle outbreaks then triggered salvage operations.

Interactive GEE dashboard for evidence-based forest management

Publicly accessible tool allowing foresters and researchers to query disturbance footprints, visualise biomass trajectories, and identify hotspot zones. No GIS software required.
